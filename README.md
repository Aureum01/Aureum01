Rough estimates from multiple industry and trade analyses show that about one-quarter to one-third of the world’s fertilizer trade moves through the Strait of Hormuz.

Key numbers:

~25–35% of global fertilizer trade passes through the strait. 

Around one-third of global fertilizer supply is tied to that route. 

About 25–30% of nitrogen fertilizer exports (ammonia, urea) move through it. 

Some estimates show ~33% of fertilizers including sulfur and ammonia transit the chokepoint. 

Breakdown by fertilizer type (approximate):

Urea: ~31% of global shipments 

Ammonia: ~18–25% of global shipments 

Phosphate inputs: ~15–20% 

Sulfur (used in fertilizer): ~30–40% 

Why this chokepoint matters

The Persian Gulf region (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE, Iran) is one of the largest fertilizer export hubs in the world. 

Most of those exports must pass through the Strait of Hormuz to reach global markets. 

If shipping stops there, global fertilizer prices spike quickly, which then pushes up food prices and crop costs worldwide. 

Short version:

Roughly 1 out of every 3 fertilizer shipments globally passes through the Strait of Hormuz.
